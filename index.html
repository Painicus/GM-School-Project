On November 26, 2018 General Motors announced a significant gash in their workforce that could prove disastrous for the North American Automotive job market. In a move that will cut 15 percent of its 54,000 salaried employees in North America as well as put approximately 6,700 hourly workers at risk of job loss; General Motors is unallocating five plants in North America in 2019. This includes assembly plants in Michigan, Ohio, and Canada as well as propulsion plants in Maryland and Michigan. Unallocation, or GM electing not to send new products to a factory, does not necessarily mean that the jobs at those factories are lost. What it does do is make bargaining for the UAW a lot dicier of a prospect come contract renewal time next year.

In response, The UAW has filed a "formal objection" to General Motors in regards both the plant closings as well as the vehicles being nixed from the line that the threatened plants already build. Gary Jones, UAW President, issued a statement along with the objection on December 3, 2018:

We have been clear that the UAW will leave no stone unturned and use any and all resources available to us regarding the future of these plants. Today, we wrote to GM formally objecting to its unilateral decision regarding four U.S. manufacturing facilities.

In justification of this move, GM states that the closure/unallocations of these plants will increase cash flow by $6 billion by the end of 2020. This will help to make GM into a "highly agile, resilient, and profitable" with more breathability in its ability to invest in new and burgeoning technologies, according to CEO and chairman Mary Barra. The AP has reports that these moves will ultimately lead to the loss of 14,700 American jobs, including 8,100 salaried white-collar workers.

The general reasonings on either side of the political spectrum are:

  • Make better cars and this wouldn't be a problem. OR
  • The tariffs and tax breaks levied by President Trump and his administration did nothing but line the pockets of the rich and encourage a shutdown due to prices.
Like all conundrums such as this, the problems (and their solutions) aren't quite as black and white as we would like them to be. Check out the other pages for some information about both lines of thinking, and make your own conclusions.
